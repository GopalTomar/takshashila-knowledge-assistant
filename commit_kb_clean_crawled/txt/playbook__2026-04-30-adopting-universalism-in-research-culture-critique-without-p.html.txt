[2026-04-30 05:50 UTC] @pranaykotas: This essay argues that good science requires critiquing ideas universally, not shielding "less powerful" scholars from rigorous public criticism.

Michael Inzlicht describes how a norm against "punching down" in academia made senior scholars wary of publicly criticizing students or marginalized researchers, even when their work was weak. He gives examples: a "hot women don't feel cold" paper and a qualitative policing study, where critics were attacked for power dynamics instead of their substantive points. He also skewers "sycophancy down," where average work by marginalized scholars gets exaggerated praise, which he sees as patronizing rather than protective.

He anchors his argument in Merton's norm of universalism: scientific claims should be judged by shared standards, irrespective of who makes them. If we avoid critiquing junior or marginalized scholars, we implicitly treat them as less-than-full participants in science, which is infantilizing. He also notes that "power" is now multi-dimensional: a grad student with a large online following or activist backing can exert real reputational power over a tenured professor, making the punch-up/punch-down frame incoherent.

At the same time, he insists criticism must be delivered with care. Drawing on the Talmudic view that public humiliation is akin to shedding blood, he distinguishes between rigorous critique and personal denigration. The right ideal, in his view, is open, public disagreement about ideas, offered civilly and kindly, with no exemptions from scrutiny based on identity or career stage. Calling for "better fights," he closes by inviting readers to criticize his own argument, regardless of their status.
