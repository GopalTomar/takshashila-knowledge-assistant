TL;DR
: A strategic approach to engaging with misinformation and opposing viewpoints in WhatsApp groups while maintaining credibility and civility.
Key Points
Make polite interventions by pointing out factual errors or logical flaws to signal alternative viewpoints exist
Use Socratic questioning to challenge claims (e.g., 'Where in the Arthashastra does it say so?')
Keep interventions brief - one or two messages maximum, avoid long arguments
Choose battles wisely and maintain credibility by praising what's good while presenting fact-based contrary views
The goal is exposure of counter-views, not winning debates or changing minds immediately
Context
This conversation emerged from the common challenge of navigating social media discussions where misinformation or partisan views dominate. Given Takshashila's focus on evidence-based policy discourse, this represents a practical guide for public intellectuals and policy practitioners engaging in online discussions.

[2024-01-25 11:30 UTC] @nitinpai: Sharing this here because quite a number of people are facing this. 

How do you handle WhatsApp groups, where a lot of people believe in things that you oppose. 

1. Make a polite intervention, pointing out why something is not based on fact, or why the argument is not based on logic etc. This is to signal to everyone that there is a different point of view, and that the majority view has been challenged. 

2. One way to do the above is to ask questions. Eg "Where in the Arthashastra does it say so?" 

3. Do not get into a long argument. One or two messages are enough. You can't 'win' this debate by debating. 

4. Chill. Get on with your life. 

The purpose is to let others know that a counter view exists.
